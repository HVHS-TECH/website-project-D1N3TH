Frequently Asked Questions

Find quick, concise answers to the most common questions about university admission, student finances, and campus life in New Zealand.

Admissions and Study

What is the difference between undergraduate and postgraduate study?

Undergraduate study is your first university qualification, typically a Bachelor's degree (e.g., Bachelor of Arts, Bachelor of Science). This is the starting point for most school leavers.

Postgraduate study is advanced study undertaken after completing a Bachelor's degree. This includes qualifications like Graduate Certificates, Master's degrees, and PhD doctorates.

Do I need NCEA Level 3 to gain University Entrance (UE)?

Yes, for most domestic students, NCEA Level 3 (with 14 credits in 3 approved subjects, 8 credits in Literacy, and 10 credits in Numeracy) is the standard pathway to gain University Entrance (UE).

However, some students may qualify through alternative pathways, such as foundation studies, completing the International Baccalaureate (IB), or A-Levels. Always check the specific entrance requirements of your preferred university.

Can I change my university course after I start?

Yes, most universities allow you to add, drop, or swap courses within the first few weeks of the semester (often called the 'add/drop' period). After this deadline, changes become much more complex and may affect your student loan and fees.

It’s essential to speak with an academic advisor or student support services immediately if you are considering changing courses or programs.

Finance and Funding

Do I have to take out a Student Loan to study at university?

No, taking out a loan is optional. However, most domestic students use the interest-free Student Loan to cover their compulsory tuition fees and course-related costs.

If you or your family can afford to pay the fees upfront, or if you secure a significant scholarship, you may choose not to use the loan. Remember, a Student Loan also covers a living cost component, which many students rely on.

What is the difference between a Student Loan and a Student Allowance?

A Student Loan is money you must pay back (interest-free if you remain in NZ). It covers tuition fees and can include a weekly living cost payment.

A Student Allowance is a tax-free grant that you do not have to pay back. It is available to eligible students based on your income and your parents' income (means-testing) and is designed to help with weekly living expenses.

When should I apply to StudyLink?

You should apply to StudyLink (for your Student Loan and/or Allowance) as early as possible. It's highly recommended to apply at least 4 to 6 weeks before your course starts to ensure your funding is processed and available when the semester begins.

Student Life and Accommodation

Is it better to stay in a Hall of Residence or a Flat in the first year?

For first-year students coming from outside the city, a Hall of Residence is generally recommended. It provides catered meals, built-in social networks, close proximity to campus, and a simplified cost structure (no separate bills).

Flatting (shared rental housing) offers more independence and is usually cheaper overall, but it is better suited for second-year students onwards who have established social connections and budgeting skills.

How much time should I spend studying outside of lectures?

A good rule of thumb for university study is to allocate 2-3 hours of self-directed study for every hour of lectures/tutorials. If you have 12 contact hours per week, you should plan for an additional 24-36 hours of independent reading, research, and assignment work.

Where can I go for academic or personal support?

Every university offers extensive free student support:

  • Academic Help: Use the Academic Learning Centres for essay writing, referencing, and study skills.
  • Personal Support: Contact the University Counselling Services or Health Services for confidential mental wellbeing or medical help.
  • General Advice: Visit the Student Hub or Student Association for administrative and general advice.
