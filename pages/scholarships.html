Scholarships

Funding Your Future: A Guide to Scholarships

Scholarships are non-repayable grants of money given to students to help fund their study costs. They are a fantastic way to ease the financial burden of university life. While they are competitive, there is a wide range available for different talents and circumstances.

1. Types of Scholarships

Scholarships are not just for straight-A students! They can be awarded based on many factors, and often cover tuition fees, accommodation, or general living costs.

  • Academic Scholarships: Awarded for excellence in NCEA (usually based on your Level 2 or 3 results), Cambridge, or IB qualifications. These are often the most competitive.
  • Sports and Cultural Scholarships: For students who have excelled in a particular sport, art, or performance area. You'll typically need a strong portfolio or evidence of high-level achievement.
  • Equity and General Scholarships: Designed to support students who may face financial hardship, come from low-decile schools, or are first in their family to attend university.
  • Maori and Pasifika Scholarships: Specific funding targeted at eligible Māori and Pasifika students to support their studies and cultural success.
  • Subject-Specific Scholarships: Often offered by a particular university department to encourage students into areas like Science, Engineering, or Health.
  • Female Scholarships: Designed to encourage women into fields like STEM (Science, Technology, Engineering, and Mathematics) where they are underrepresented, or to support women returning to tertiary study
    • Pro Tip: Don't limit yourself to university scholarships. Check with your high school, local trusts, and even private companies (e.g., banks, major industries) for external funding opportunities.

    2. Where to Find Scholarships

    Finding the right scholarship takes time and research. Start early—many university scholarship applications close in August to October of your final high school year!

    • University Websites: Every NZ university maintains a comprehensive database of scholarships they offer, including criteria and deadlines. This should be your primary source.
    • External Databases: Websites like givME (often accessible through your school or public library) list thousands of scholarships from various private trusts and organisations
    • Your High School: Your Dean, Careers Advisor, or Principal's Nominee often have inside knowledge on local and school-specific scholarship funds.

    3. Essential Application Tips

    A strong application is key to securing funding. Treat it like a job application—you're competing for a limited resource.

    1. Start Early: Gather necessary documents (CVs, transcripts, financial statements) well before the deadline.
    2. Personal Statement: This is crucial. Tailor your essay to the specific scholarship criteria. Be authentic, highlight your achievements, and clearly articulate how the scholarship will help you succeed.
    3. Recommendation Letters: Give your referees (teachers, coaches, employers) plenty of notice and provide them with all the information they need about the scholarship.
    4. Proofread Everything: Typos and mistakes suggest a lack of care. Have multiple people review your application before submitting.
